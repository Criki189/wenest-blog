You open the Energy Australia bill in late February and the number is $640 for one month. The ducted aircon ran most of January. You expected high, but not this high.

By the end of this article, you'll know the real per-hour cost for your system size, what drives the number up or down, and whether ducted is cheaper than running three split systems.

## What ducted air conditioning actually costs per hour in Sydney

A typical 12kW ducted system in a three-bedroom Sydney home costs **$0.80 to $1.20 per hour** to run, assuming 30 cents per kWh on your electricity plan. That's the rate most Sydney households pay during summer peak periods.

The range depends on how hard the system works. On a 28-degree day with good ceiling insulation, the compressor cycles off every 15 minutes. On a 38-degree day with no insulation and west-facing windows, it runs continuously. The continuous scenario pushes toward $1.20 per hour. The cycling scenario sits closer to $0.80.

For an 18kW system in a larger home — four bedrooms, two storeys, 250 square metres — expect $1.20 to $1.80 per hour under the same conditions.

Run the system eight hours per day for 60 days across summer. At $1 per hour average, that's $480 for the season. At $1.50 per hour, it's $720. Most Sydney homes land between those two numbers.

## How your system size affects the bill

Ducted systems are rated in kilowatts of cooling capacity. The compressor outside draws roughly 2.5 to 3.5 kilowatts of electricity for every 10 kilowatts of cooling output. That ratio is the energy efficiency rating, usually between 3.0 and 4.0 for mid-range systems installed in the last decade.

Here's what common system sizes cost to run per hour at 30c per kWh:

- **10kW system** (small three-bedroom home, 120-150 sqm) — 2.5 to 3.3 kW draw — **$0.75 to $1.00 per hour**
- **12kW system** (standard three-bedroom, 150-180 sqm) — 3.0 to 4.0 kW draw — **$0.90 to $1.20 per hour**
- **14kW system** (large three-bedroom or small four-bedroom, 180-220 sqm) — 3.5 to 4.7 kW draw — **$1.05 to $1.40 per hour**
- **18kW system** (four-bedroom two-storey, 220-280 sqm) — 4.5 to 6.0 kW draw — **$1.35 to $1.80 per hour**

If you don't know your system size, check the outdoor compressor label or the original invoice. The number is almost always on the compliance plate.

## Ducted vs split systems: the cost comparison

Three 2.5kW split systems — one in the master bedroom, one in the living room, one in a second bedroom — draw roughly 0.8 kilowatts each when running. Total: 2.4 kilowatts. At 30c per kWh, that's **$0.72 per hour** if all three are on.

The same home with a 12kW ducted system costs $0.90 to $1.20 per hour to cool the entire house. If you're only using three rooms, the ducted system still cools hallways, the kitchen, and closed bedrooms unless you have zoning.

The split system setup wins on running cost if you only cool occupied rooms. The ducted system wins on whole-house comfort, resale value, and aesthetics — no wall-mounted units in every room.

In the homes we work in across the Eastern Suburbs and Inner West, the split system approach saves $200 to $400 per summer for families who close off unused rooms and don't mind managing three remotes. The ducted approach costs more to run but delivers even temperature across the house and better air circulation.

## What actually drives your ducted aircon power bill

Four factors account for 80% of the variation between a $400 summer and a $700 summer:

### Ceiling insulation

A well-insulated ceiling keeps cool air in and radiant heat out. The system cycles off for 10 to 15 minutes every half hour instead of running continuously. That difference alone cuts the bill by 20% to 30%.

Most homes built before 2005 in Sydney have minimal or degraded ceiling batts. Adding R3.5 or R4.0 polyester batts costs $1,800 to $2,800 for a three-bedroom home and pays itself back in two to three summers.

### Thermostat setting

Every degree below 24°C increases energy use by roughly 10%. Setting the thermostat to 22°C instead of 24°C adds $0.10 to $0.15 per hour. Over 480 hours across summer, that's $48 to $72.

The sweet spot for comfort and cost in Sydney is 24°C to 25°C. Below 23°C, you're paying for diminishing returns.

### Zoning (and whether you're using it correctly)

Most ducted systems installed in the last 15 years have zoning — the ability to close vents in unused rooms via a wall controller. The theory is you save energy by cooling less space.

The reality is more complicated. Closing too many zones forces the compressor to work harder against higher static pressure. If you close off more than 40% of the vents, the system often uses *more* energy, not less.

The optimal approach: close vents in one or two unused bedrooms, but leave at least 60% of the house open. Never close all zones except one room — the system isn't designed for that.

### Time of day and electricity plan

If you're on a time-of-use plan, running the aircon between 2 PM and 8 PM costs 35c to 50c per kWh. Running it between 10 PM and 7 AM costs 15c to 20c per kWh. That's a 2x to 3x difference.

Pre-cooling the house at 6 AM before leaving for work, then turning the system off until 6 PM, can cut the daily cost by $2 to $4. Over summer, that's $120 to $240.

## The Wenest take

In the homes we work in, the version of this that actually fails is the "leave it on all day at 26°C" strategy. The idea is the system won't have to work as hard to recover from a hot house at 5 PM.

For modern inverter systems, that's false economy. You're paying for six to eight hours of compressor time you don't need. Turn it off when you leave. Turn it on 30 minutes before you get home. You'll save $4 to $6 per day without sacrificing comfort.

The second mistake is closing vents in every unused room. We've measured power draw in a dozen homes where closing 60% of the zones increased the bill by 15% to 20%. The compressor runs hotter and cycles less efficiently. Leave most zones open unless you have a modern variable-speed system explicitly designed for heavy zoning.

## How to estimate your actual cost

Find your system size (check the outdoor unit label or the original invoice). Find your electricity rate (check your last bill — it's usually 28c to 35c per kWh for Sydney households on a standard plan).

Use this formula:

**System size in kW ÷ efficiency rating × electricity rate = cost per hour**

Example: 12kW system, 3.5 efficiency rating, 30c per kWh.

12 ÷ 3.5 = 3.4 kW draw  
3.4 × $0.30 = **$1.02 per hour**

That's the maximum if the compressor runs continuously. In practice, the system cycles on and off, so multiply by 0.7 to 0.9 depending on insulation and outside temperature. For this example, expect $0.71 to $0.92 per hour in real conditions.

Track one week of usage. Note the hours the system ran each day and compare to your meter reading or app. That gives you the real number for your house.

## Frequently asked questions

### How much does it cost to run ducted aircon for 8 hours in Sydney?

For an average 12kW system in a three-bedroom Sydney home, expect $6.40 to $9.60 for eight hours at 30c per kWh. That assumes the system cycles on and off rather than running flat out. A poorly insulated home or 38-degree day will push toward the higher end.

### Is ducted air conditioning cheaper to run than split systems?

Not usually. A ducted system cooling the whole house costs $0.80 to $1.20 per hour. Three split systems in bedrooms and living areas cost roughly $0.50 to $0.70 per hour combined. The gap widens if you only cool rooms you're using. Ducted wins on comfort and resale appeal, not running cost.

### What's the biggest factor in ducted aircon running costs?

Insulation. A well-insulated ceiling keeps the system cycling off for longer periods. In homes we work in across the North Shore, adding ceiling batts dropped summer bills by 20% to 30%. Zoning comes second — closing vents in unused rooms forces the system to work harder, not less.

### Does leaving ducted aircon on all day save money?

No. The myth that restarting costs more than leaving it on doesn't hold for modern inverter systems. Turn it off when you leave for work. If you're out for six hours, you'll save $4 to $6 per day. Over a Sydney summer, that's $300 to $500.

### How do I know if my ducted system is using too much power?

Compare your summer electricity bill to your winter bill. If the difference is more than $400 for a three-bedroom home, the system is likely oversized, under-maintained, or fighting poor insulation.
