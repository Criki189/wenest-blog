A blocked sink is the most outsourceable problem people don't outsource. It looks small, it feels small, and yet 9 out of 10 times you can fix it in the next twenty minutes without spending $180 on a plumber callout.

Here are the five things to try, in the order we'd actually try them.

## 1. Clear the trap manually

Under the sink, that U-shaped pipe is called the P-trap. It catches things. In a Sydney kitchen, "things" usually means coffee grounds, rice, and a slow build-up of grease.

Put a bucket underneath. Unscrew the two collars on either side of the U by hand — they should be finger-tight. Pull the trap down, tip the contents into the bucket, run a brush or your finger through it, and screw it back on.

This is the fix about 60% of the time. It also costs nothing.

## 2. Boil the kettle

Grease is the silent killer of Sydney drains, especially in flats. Even when you think you're not pouring oil down the sink, you are — every time you rinse a frying pan.

Boil a full kettle. Pour it down in three stages, 30 seconds between each. The hot water re-melts the grease and pushes it through.

Caveat: don't do this if you've got old PVC pipes. If you're not sure, use very hot tap water instead.

## 3. Plunge it properly

Most people plunge wrong. Two rules:

- **Cover the overflow.** That little hole near the top of the basin? It breaks your seal. Stuff a wet cloth into it.
- **Push down hard, pull up harder.** The pull is what dislodges the blockage, not the push. Five or six aggressive pumps, then check.

Use a flat-cup plunger (the cheap red ones), not a flange plunger — flange ones are for toilets.

## 4. Bicarb and vinegar (and salt)

This is the one that lives on Pinterest. It's real, but it's not a miracle.

- Pour half a cup of bicarb soda down the drain.
- Follow with half a cup of salt.
- Then a cup of white vinegar.
- Cover the drain with a plate.
- Leave for 15 minutes.
- Flush with hot water.

It works on mild blockages and is excellent monthly maintenance. It's not going to rescue a sink that's already full.

## 5. The drain snake

If you've made it to step five, you're past the easy stuff. Buy or borrow a hand-crank drain snake — about $25 at Bunnings. Feed it down the pipe past the trap, crank gently when you feel resistance, then pull it out (with the gross stuff attached).

Wear gloves. Have the bucket. Maybe open a window.

## When to stop

There's exactly one situation where you should put the tools down and call someone:

> The blockage keeps coming back, or you smell sewage, or water comes up into another fixture.

That's not a sink problem. That's a line problem — somewhere between your house and the main. You can snake from now until Sunday and you won't reach it. Call a plumber with a CCTV camera.

## What we'd do as Wenest

For our members, blocked sinks are usually a five-minute phone call. Nine times out of ten we walk them through steps 1-3 from this article and the problem is gone before the kettle's cool.

## A quick prevention list

Avoid step 1-5 entirely with three habits:

- A drain strainer in the sink. $4. Lasts forever.
- Never tip cooking oil down the drain. Pour it into an empty bottle, bin it.
- Once a month, the bicarb-vinegar-hot-water flush. Takes three minutes.

Most kitchen sink problems are a slow accumulation, not a sudden event. The maintenance is laughably cheap; the callout is not.
