The water won't drain. You've just washed up after dinner and the sink is half-full of greasy water that's going nowhere. You'll try to unblock a kitchen sink yourself before spending $180 on a callout.

By the end of this, you'll know the four methods that actually work in Sydney homes, in order of effort, and exactly when to stop and book a plumber.

## What's blocking your kitchen sink

Most kitchen sink blockages sit in the P-trap — the U-shaped pipe directly beneath the sink. Food scraps, coffee grounds, congealed fat, and soap scum accumulate there over weeks. The trap is designed to hold water as a seal against sewer gases, but that same water slows drainage when debris builds up.

Less common: the blockage is further down the waste line, past the trap, where the pipe connects to the main stack. Tree roots, collapsed pipes, or a shared blockage with other fixtures point to this. If your bathroom sink or shower is also draining slowly, the problem is not in the kitchen trap.

## Method 1: Boiling water and detergent

Start here if the sink is draining slowly but not completely stopped.

Boil a full kettle. Squirt 100ml of dishwashing liquid directly into the drain. Wait two minutes for the detergent to work into the blockage. Pour the boiling water in one steady stream, not in stages. The heat liquefies fat, the detergent breaks surface tension, and the volume flushes debris through.

This works on grease-based blockages in about 40% of cases. If the water sits in the sink without draining at all, move to the plunger.

One warning: if you have PVC pipes and they're older than 15 years, boiling water can soften joints. Most Sydney homes built after 2000 have PVC rated to 80°C, but pre-1990 installations sometimes used thinner-wall pipe. If you're unsure, use hot tap water instead of boiling.

## Method 2: Plunger

You need a flat-cup sink plunger, not a toilet plunger. Fill the sink with 8-10cm of water — enough to cover the plunger cup. If you have a double sink, block the second drain with a wet cloth or the drain plug. This prevents air escaping through the other side and breaking the vacuum.

Place the plunger over the drain. Push down slowly to expel air, then pull up sharply. The suction on the upstroke dislodges blockages more than the downward push. Repeat 15-20 times in a steady rhythm. You'll feel resistance change when the blockage shifts.

Run hot water for 30 seconds after the sink drains to flush remaining debris. If the water drains but slowly, repeat the process. If there's no change after three rounds, the blockage is either too solid or too far down the line for a plunger to reach.

## Method 3: Remove and clean the P-trap

This is the most reliable DIY method because you're physically removing the section where 80% of blockages sit.

Place a bucket beneath the trap. The trap holds roughly 500ml of water, sometimes more if the sink was full. Unscrew the two slip nuts by hand — one connects the trap to the sink tailpiece, the other connects to the wall outlet. If they're too tight, use a wrench but wrap the jaws in a cloth to avoid scratching chrome.

Pull the trap away. Tip the contents into the bucket. You'll see a mix of water, food scraps, and grey sludge. Run a bottle brush or old toothbrush through the trap to clear residue from the inner walls. Rinse under an outdoor tap or in the laundry sink.

Check the trap for cracks while it's off. PVC traps crack at the threads after 10-15 years, especially if they've been overtightened. A replacement trap costs $12-$18 at Bunnings and takes five minutes to install.

Reassemble in reverse order. Hand-tighten the slip nuts — they don't need to be wrenched. Run water and check for leaks at both joints. If water drips, tighten another quarter-turn.

If the sink still won't drain after cleaning the trap, the blockage is in the waste line beyond the trap. That requires either a drain snake or a plumber.

## Method 4: Drain snake (use with caution)

A hand-crank drain snake costs $25-$40 and reaches blockages up to 3 metres down the waste line. Feed the cable into the drain past the trap until you feel resistance. Crank clockwise while pushing gently. The auger at the tip either breaks up the blockage or hooks onto it so you can pull it back.

This works on soft blockages — hair, soap, food — but not on tree roots or collapsed pipes. If you feel hard resistance that won't shift, stop. Forcing a snake through a damaged pipe can widen cracks or punch through a rusted section.

We've been called to roughly 30 jobs in the last year where a snake caused more damage than the original blockage. The risk is highest in homes built before 1980 with cast iron or galvanised steel waste pipes. If you're not confident about what's in your walls, skip this step.

## When to stop and call a plumber

Book a licensed plumber if any of these apply:

- The blockage recurs within two weeks of clearing it.
- Multiple fixtures are draining slowly (bathroom sink, shower, laundry).
- You hear gurgling from other drains when the kitchen sink drains.
- Water backs up into the dishwasher or washing machine.
- You've removed the trap and the waste line beyond is clear, but the sink still won't drain.
- The building is a strata unit and the blockage might be in shared pipework.

These point to blockages in the main waste line or sewer connection. A plumber will use a camera to locate the obstruction and either jet or excavate depending on what's found. Cost in Sydney: $280-$450 for a camera inspection, $150-$180 per metre for jetting, $2,000-$5,000 for excavation if tree roots have collapsed the pipe.

In the homes we work in across Sydney, the version of this that actually fails is assuming the blockage is always in the trap. We've attended roughly 60 blocked sink jobs in the last 12 months. About 40% were in the trap and cleared with the methods above. The other 60% were further down the line — tree roots in the sewer (Inner West terraces, North Shore homes near Moreton Bay figs), collapsed earthenware pipes (pre-1960 builds), or shared blockages in strata buildings where one unit's grease affects three others.

The trap is always worth checking first because it's the easiest fix. But if you've cleaned it twice and the problem returns, you're not dealing with a trap issue.

## Frequently asked questions

### How long does it take to unblock a kitchen sink with a plunger?

Most kitchen sink blockages clear within 5-10 minutes of plunging if the blockage is in the trap directly beneath the sink. If you're not seeing progress after 15 minutes of consistent effort, the blockage is likely further down the line or requires a different approach.

### Can I use Drano or chemical drain cleaners in my kitchen sink?

Chemical drain cleaners work on some blockages but can damage older pipes, especially if you have PVC under 40mm diameter or mixed pipe materials. They're also hazardous if they don't clear the blockage and you need to plunge afterward. We see more pipe damage from chemicals than from blockages themselves in Sydney homes built before 1990.

### Why does my kitchen sink keep blocking even after I clear it?

Recurring blockages usually mean either a partial obstruction further down the waste line, inadequate fall in the pipework, or tree roots in the sewer line. In Inner West terraces, we find tree roots in roughly 60% of recurring drainage issues. A camera inspection is the only way to know for certain.

### What's the difference between a sink plunger and a toilet plunger?

A sink plunger has a flat cup, while a toilet plunger has a folded flange. Using a toilet plunger on a sink won't create a proper seal. Most Sydney hardware stores sell combination plungers that work for both, but a dedicated sink plunger costs $8-$15 and works better.
